## Thesis

Treat WordPress Playground exactly like a non-linear video editor. The WordPress iframe is the **program monitor** at the top of the screen — always full-bleed, always in focus. Every other control is exiled to a **resizable bay** along the bottom: a left "browser" rail for sources (saved playgrounds + ways-to-start), a center "timeline" of blueprint boot steps and logs, and a right "inspector" panel that hot-swaps between Settings, Files, Blueprint JSON, and Database. A transport strip directly under the monitor handles refresh, address, save, name, settings popover, and Site Manager toggle — the way a DAW or Premiere panel has scrub controls under the preview. The user always sees the running site; the controls bow to it.

## What I remixed / merged / removed

I **merged** the Site Manager's five tabs into the bottom-right Inspector — it's the same content (Settings, Files, Blueprint, Database, Logs), but framed as a context-sensitive inspector pane that swaps via tabs, not a separate sidebar with its own header. The **Saved Playgrounds launcher** and **Site Manager** are no longer two separate panels; "Your Playgrounds," "Start a new Playground," and the full 43-blueprint gallery all live in the left rail "Browser," with the gallery expanding to fill the bay when you click "Browse all 43." The transport bar **collapses the three settings entry points** into one popover triggered by a gear icon. The bottom bay is **resizable** (drag the divider) and **collapsible** (one click hides everything, giving the iframe the entire viewport — the "full-screen monitor" mode every editor has). Bottom-left also hosts the toast region so notifications appear from the control bay, not floating randomly. Nothing is dropped: every flow listed in the brief is reachable in two clicks from the transport bar.

## Trade-offs

The metaphor is dense — first-time visitors will see more chrome than the current Playground shows them, and the timeline-style step viewer is more legible for blueprint authors than for someone who just wants "Vanilla WordPress, please" (they're served by the big "+" button in the rail, but it's not the loudest thing on the screen). On mobile the two-up split has to collapse into a vertical stack with a bottom-sheet bay, which loses the side-by-side timeline feel and reverts to a more conventional drawer pattern. The Inspector hot-swapping behavior means you can't see Files and Logs at the same time — power users who want a split inspector would need a "pop out" feature I didn't add (it would have been a new feature, which the brief forbids).
