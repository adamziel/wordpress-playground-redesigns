# Single-page scroll

## Thesis
The chrome around the WordPress iframe is reorganized as a long, editorial document on the left, with the live WordPress site pinned as a sticky preview on the right. There are no modals, no drawers, no tabs — every Playground surface (quick start, blueprint gallery, your saved sites, settings, files, blueprint JSON, database, logs) is a section you scroll into. A slim left-edge rail shows your position in the document and lets you jump. The mental model becomes "this Playground is a single page about your Playground", which removes the three-overlapping-settings-screens confusion of the current UI and lets the iframe remain visible at all times — you can tweak settings and see the result without ever closing a panel.

## What I remixed / merged / removed
The launcher drawer, the site manager sidebar, the settings popover, the additional-actions menu, and every modal are merged into one document. The five Site Manager tabs become five sequential sections (Settings, Files, Blueprint, Database, Logs), not tabs. The Settings popover and the Settings tab collapse into one "Settings" section — there is no longer a duplicate quick-popover. "Start a new Playground", "Start from a Blueprint", and "Your Playgrounds" sit one above the other as section 1, 2, 3 of the document; the 5-featured-plus-view-all split is gone — featured tiles and the full 43-card gallery with category filters and search live in the same section, with featured pinned to the top of the same grid. "Additional actions" (Export PR, Download zip) is no longer a hidden menu; it lives as two big buttons next to "WP Admin / Homepage" in the section header. Toasts stay (bottom-right). The marketing "Hello from WordPress Playground!" landing inside the iframe is replaced by the document itself.

## Trade-offs
Vertical scrolling becomes the only navigation, which is great for discovery (newcomers literally cannot miss the 43 blueprints) and terrible for power users who already know exactly where to go — that's why the left rail anchors-jumps are sized big and given keystrokes (1–9). The sticky iframe takes ~55% of desktop width, which is generous but means the document column is narrower than a typical settings page — multi-file editing in the Blueprint section is therefore a single-pane editor with a thinner file column, not a 50/50 IDE. On mobile, "sticky" becomes impossible without sacrificing reading width, so the iframe collapses to a sticky 220 px-tall card at the top of the viewport and the rail becomes a horizontal scrollchip strip. The "Apply Settings & Reset" action is destructive and the inline scroll model means users could trigger it while skimming — it's gated by a confirm step in the section itself.
